# Selling looted arms and armor

## Recovering armor: 
When someone is slain, the armor he wears ends up pierced, dented, and otherwise compromised. Armor recovered from a fallen foe (or ally) has a 25% chance of being useless. It can be repaired by an armorer, typically for one-quarter to one-half the original cost of the armor. Demi-humans are of an unusual physiognomy and cannot wear armor sized for a human. If not otherwise specified, there is a 75% chance that randomly discovered armor is human-sized. 

## Recovering missile fire weapons: 
Characters can recover missile weapons that miss their target. There is a 50% chance that recovered arrows, sling stones, javelins, and other weapons are destroyed; otherwise, they can be reused. 
## Scavenging

Weapons and armor sell for 1/2 their listed price
- Scavengers take 1/3 of the proceeds
- = 1/3 listed value

# Weapons and Armor

Weapons and armor have always evolved alongside each other in a lasting, evolutionary struggle. A Sword is a great weapon against an unarmored peasant, but a middling one against a knight in full plate. As such, the ruleset should reflect the tactical choice of weapon against an opponent.

### Armor Class Vector
Armor has 4 AC scores:
- Base
- Piercing
- Slashing
- Bludgeoning

Each of the damage types are variants from the base in the rulebook. Those categorizations are here:
AC vs damage type: [[armor_bonus.png]]


for example, leather armor has a base bonus of +2, for a standard AC of 12
The full vector looks like:
Base: 12
Piercing: 10
Slashing: 12
bludgeoning: 12

Thus, an opponent facing a foe in leather armor has a +10% better chance to wound him if he opts for a piercing weapon over other types, representing the unique weakness the armor type

## Storytelling and META

Most experienced players would immediately note that in this paradigm they should immediately go for the technologically superior armor, and that is true for most of human history. However, the availability of different armor types is a function of economic circumstances, technology available in the area, and the tactical choices of the combatants. Heavy, plate knights in Europe coincided with heavy lamellar laden cataphracts in north africa. 

Armor speaks more about the culture it arose from than the mechanics of a game, and note that heavier armors come with movement penalties, skill check penalities, and higher fumble rolls.


# Special Weapon Rules

Weapons have damage types that line up against different armor classes, which informs weapon choice. A warhammer is better against a plate-armored opponent, and a sword is best against a unarmored peasant. see rules for armor vector. The damage type listed reflects the primary use of the weapon, not the only one. You can imagine a sword is used for cutting and thrusting, but if it is optimized for the cut we classify it to that damage type to preserve game balance. Some advanced weapons like multi-headed polearms can be included but should be rare for how they break the rules of the game.

## Armor

Armors can be generally thought of in terms of coverage, materials, and construction.

Light armors typically cover only the torso, medium armors include a helmet and some limb protection, heavy armors cover everything.

Material and construction typically denote the armor vector. A cuirass of chain links will protect you well against slashes and stabs, but less against a bludgeon. A cuirass of steel would protect you well against all 3, but also be less flexible in battle.

The more you wear, the slower you are (movement penalties, agility modifier penalties) and the more prone to fumbles

# Hirelings
**Hirelings**

Most heroes aren’t looking to just hire just any rutabaga farmer with a sharpened stick, they want blacksmiths, mercenaries, guards and alchemists.

If you are looking for an unskilled hireling, with no special qualities or background, to serve as porters or menial labour, 1 in 100 people in a town are available for hire, with no special checks.  If you are looking for fleshing out your hireling, roll 3d6 for each of their statistics, and roll for a background as per the DCC rulebook, and start them with 1d4 hp and 1d12 cp.  No birth augury or special equipment should be rolled like you would give a 0-level player character.  These hirelings expect 1 cp per day (1 sp per day if they are placed in dangerous situations).

[[DCC - Core Book.pdf#page=311|DCC - Core Book, p.311]]

Adventurers of wealth and renown recruit hirelings and henchmen to explore with them. Danger and excitement do not motivate all men, and thus these retainers must be promised a fee or a share of treasures recovered. An adventuring party may hire on retainers using these rules. Note, however, that not all retainers may wish to travel. Many want only to earn a few coppers to feed their families. Do not assume that adventurers can keep the same hired hands from one locale to another. 

**Recruiting**: For every 100 residents of a town or village, 1 man—not necessarily able-bodied—is willing to risk his life as an adventurer’s helper. Areas of famine or hardship, where there are no other opportunities, may produce more prospective retainers; bustling centers of trade, with many competing chances at wealth, may produce fewer. 

**Equipping**: Retainers come equipped with simple hide or leather armor, caps, and an axe, club, or spear. Some 20% have their own longswords and possibly armor but expect extra payment for being so equipped. All other equipment must be supplied by the characters. 

**Payment** : Simple military duty in mundane circumstances, such as a man-at-arms in an army, commands payment of food and lodging plus 1-4 copper pieces per week. If the retainers bring good equipment, they’ll charge another 1-4 cp per week. A retainer expected to face supernatural horrors, un-dead spirits, or ferocious monsters demands at least 1 silver piece per day and up to 1 gold piece per day if facing extraordinary challenges. Retainers higher than level 0 are rare, but if recruited they will ask for double the above wages. Upon seeing the treasures recovered, retainers may also ask for a share thereof. 

**Morale**: As covered in the combat section, a retainer must check morale in his first encounter. It is quite possible that he will run away upon realizing what he’s up against. Unlike with normal monsters, the retainer is affected by loyalty to his employer. A retainer’s morale check is modified by his Will save and also by his employer’s Personality modifier. 

At the end of each adventure, a retainer must make another morale check. The judge applies a modifier from -4 to +4, reflecting the retainer’s wounds, his treatment at the hands of the characters, his payment and share of treasure, and the party’s general success. A retainer who fails this morale check is spooked by the life of an adventurer, and chooses to return to the toil of village life. 

**Statistics**: Retainers are level 0 characters. They have average ability scores, 3 hit points, and +0 saving throws.
